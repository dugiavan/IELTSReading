READING PASSAGE 1
Learning to Walk

These days the feet of a typical city dweller rarely encounter terrain bất kỳ more uneven than a crack in the pavement. While that may not seem like a problem, it turns out that by flattening our urban environment we have put ourselves at risk of a surprising number of chronic illnesses and disabilities. Fortunately, the commercial market has come to the rescue with a choice of products.

Research into the idea that flat floors could be detrimental to our health was pioneered back in the late 1960s. Podiatrist Charles Brantingham and physiologist Bruce Beekman were concerned with the growing epidemic of high blood pressure and reckoned they might be linked to the uniformity of the surfaces we walk on.

The trouble, they believed, was that walking continuously on flat floors concentrates forces on just a few areas of the foot. As a result, these surfaces are far more conducive to chronic stress syndromes than natural surfaces. They understood that the anatomy of the foot parallels that of the human hand and that modern lifestyles waste all this potential flexibility.

Brantingham and Beekman became convinced that the damage could be rectified by making people wobble. They tested this by having factory workers stand on spongy mats with varying degrees of resistance. This simple intervention made a huge difference; it activated a host of muscles in their legs, which in turn helped pump blood back to their hearts and reduced stress on the circulation.

Earlier this year, John Fisher and colleagues designed a mat intended to replicate the effect of walking on cobblestones. After 16 weeks, participants showed marked improvements in mobility and a significant reduction in blood pressure. The mats are now available for purchase and production is being scaled up.

In China, spas and factories promote cobblestone paths as healthful amenities. People in Europe are also embracing the idea, visiting "barefoot parks" to walk on mud, logs, and moss. If none of these appeal, a new shoe called Masai Barefoot Technology offers another option. These shoes have rounded soles that cause you to rock slightly when you stand still, exercising the small muscles around the ankle responsible for stability.

If the urban environment is detrimental to our health, a far simpler alternative is spending time in the countryside. However, for many citizens, the countryside is a dwindling resource as our concrete cities grow at a terrifying rate.

READING PASSAGE 2
Yawning

When Robert R Provine began studying yawning in the 1960s, it was difficult for him to convince research students of the merits of ‘yawning science’. Although it may appear quirky, his research was a logical extension of developmental neuroscience.

The verb ‘to yawn’ is derived from the Old English ganien, meaning to gape or open wide. Provine studied yawns by using a variation of the contagion response, asking people to 'think about yawning' to trigger the act.

Provine’s early discoveries: the yawn is highly stereotyped but not invariant in its duration. It is an excellent example of the instinctive fixed action pattern. Once started, a yawn progresses with the inevitability of a sneeze.

There are no half-yawns; this is why you cannot stifle a yawn. Yawns can come in bouts with a highly variable inter-yawn interval of about 68 seconds.

To understand its function, he tested three informative variants:
1. The closed nose yawn: Subjects could still perform normal yawns, proving the nostrils are not essential.
2. The clenched teeth yawn: By asking subjects to clench their teeth, they felt "stuck" mid-yawn. This shows that gaping of the jaws is a necessary component.
3. The nose yawn: Unlike normal breathing, yawning is impossible via nasal inhalation alone.

Yawning is a powerful movement that involves stretching facial muscles, tilting the head back, and even producing tears. It often shares properties with stretching, forming a "global motor complex".

A fascinating observation involves stroke patients paralyzed on one side. When they yawn, their paralyzed arm may rise and flex automatically—an associated response. This suggests yawning activates unconscious connections between the brain and the motor system.

Ultimately, yawning seems associated with a change of behavioral state, switching from one activity to another. It is a reminder of the ancient behavior that lurks beneath the veneer of culture and language.

READING PASSAGE 3
Endangered Languages

‘Nevermind whales, save the languages’, says Peter Monaghan. Worried about the loss of rain forests and the ozone layer? Well, neither of those is doing any worse than the 6,000 to 7,000 languages that remain in use on Earth. One half of the survivors will almost certainly be gone by 2050, while 40% more will probably be well on their way out. In their place, almost all humans will speak one of a handful of megalanguages – Mandarin, English, Spanish.

Linguists know what causes languages to disappear, but less often remarked is what happens on the way to disappearance: languages’ vocabularies, grammars and expressive potential all diminish. When a community goes over from a traditional language to a creole, they leave behind a very fine vocabulary for the landscape. As speakers become less able to express the wealth of knowledge that has filled ancestors’ lives, communities tend to become demoralised.

If the losses are so huge, why are relatively few linguists combating the situation? Australian linguists have achieved a great deal in terms of preserving languages, resulting in good documentation. In England, Peter Austin has directed efforts to limit language loss. However, at linguistics meetings in the US, there is growing evidence that not all approaches will be particularly helpful. Some linguists boast of more and more sophisticated means of capturing languages: digital recording and storage.

But these encourage the "quick dash" style of recording trip. That’s not quite what some specialists have been seeking for 30 years. Michael Krauss complains that linguists are playing with non-essentials while most of their raw data is disappearing. Who is to blame? Many blame those obsessed with **Noam Chomsky’s** approaches. Theoretical linguists have had so much influence that the profession has largely ignored the continuing disappearance of languages.

Chomsky’s landmark work of 1957 argues that all languages exhibit certain universal grammatical features, encoded in the human mind. Creating dictionaries and grammars requires prolonged and dedicated work. Documentary linguists must observe not only structural subtleties, but also social and political factors. Such work calls for persistent funding for field scientists who may venture into harsh and even hazardous places.

Once there, they may face difficulties such as community suspicion. Parents may give up using the language with their children, believing they will benefit from speaking a more widely understood one. The highest barrier to language preservation is the profession's emphasis on theory, which wears down the enthusiasm of linguists. While Chomsky has recently spoken in support of preservation, his argument remains unsentimental: the loss of a language is a tragedy because it means the permanent loss of the most relevant data. However, Nick Evans objects, arguing that the process of immersion in a language is the most demanding intellectual task.

READING PASSAGE 4
The Ant and the Mandarin

In 1476, the farmers of Berne in Switzerland decided there was only one way to rid their fields of the cutworms attacking their crops. They took the pests to court. The worms were tried, found guilty and excommunicated by the arch­bishop. In China, farmers had a more practical approach to pest control. Rather than relying on divine intervention, they put their faith in frogs, ducks and ants.

Frogs and ducks were encouraged to snap up the pests in the paddies and the occasional plague of locusts. But the notion of biological control began with an ant. More specifically, it started with the predatory yellow citrus ant, which has been polishing off pests in the orange groves of southern China for ít nhất 1,700 years. The yellow citrus ant is a type of weaver ant, which binds leaves and twigs with silk to form a neat, tent-like nest. In the beginning, farmers made do with the odd ants’ nests here and there. But it wasn’t long before growing demand led to the development of a thriving trade in nests and a new type of agriculture – ant farming.

For an insect that bites, the yellow citrus ant is remarkably popular. Even by ant standards, it is a fearsome predator. It’s big, runs fast and has a powerful nip – painful to humans but lethal to many of the insects that plague the orange groves. For ít nhất 17 centuries, Chinese orange growers have harnessed những six-legged killing machines to keep their fruit groves healthy and productive.

Citrus fruits evolved in the Far East and the Chinese discovered the delights of their flesh early on. As the ancestral home of oranges, lemons and pomelos, China also has the greatest diversity of citrus pests. And the trees that produce the sweetest fruits, the mandarins – or kan – attract a host of plant-eating insects, from sap-sucking mealy bugs to leaf-devouring caterpillars. Với so many enemies, fruit growers clearly had to have some way of protecting their orchards.

The West did not discover the Chinese orange growers’ secret weapon cho đến the early 20th century. At the time, Florida was suffering an epidemic of citrus canker. Walter Swingle, a plant physiologist working for the US Department of Agriculture, was sent to China in search of varieties of orange that were resistant to the disease. Swingle spent some time studying the citrus orchards around Guangzhou, and there he came across the story of the cultivated ant. These ants, he was told, were “grown” by the people of a small village nearby who sold them to the orange growers by the nestful.

The earliest report of citrus ants at work among the orange trees appeared in a book on botany written by Hsi Han in AD 304. “The people of Chiao-Chih sell in their markets ants in bags of rush matting. In the south, if the kan trees do not have this kind of ant, the fruits will all be damaged by many harmful insects, and not a single fruit will be perfect.” Initially, farmers relied on nests collected from the wild or bought in the market where trade in nests was brisk.

The business quickly became more sophisticated. From the 10th century, country people began to trap ants in artificial nests baited with fat. “Fruit-growing families buy these ants from vendors who make a business of collecting and selling such creatures,” wrote Chuang Chi-Yu in 1130. They wait until the ants have migrated into the bladders and take them away. By the 17th century, growers were building bamboo walkways between their trees to speed the colonisation of their orchards.

The orange growers clearly thought it worked. One authority stressed how essential it was to keep the fruit trees free of insect pests. “But hand labour is not nearly as efficient as ant power…” Swingle was just as impressed. Yet despite his reports, many Western biologists were sceptical. In the West, the idea of using one insect to destroy another was new and highly controversial. The first breakthrough had come in 1888, when the infant orange industry in California had been saved từ extinction by the Australian vedalia beetle.

The long tradition of ants in Chinese orchards only began to waver in the 1950s and 1960s with the introduction of powerful organic insecticides. Although most fruit growers switched to chemicals, those who abandoned ants in favour of chemicals quickly became disillusioned. As costs soared and pests began to develop resistance to the chemicals, growers began to revive the old ant patrols. They had good reason to have faith in their insect workforce.

Research showed that as long as there were enough ants, they did an excellent job of dispatching some pests. Trees with yellow ants produced almost 20 per cent more healthy leaves. More recent trials have shown that những trees yield just as big a crop as những protected by expensive chemical sprays.

One apparent drawback was that citrus ants protect mealy bugs in exchange for the sweet honey-dew they secrete. Where mealy bugs proliferate under the ants’ protection, they are usually heavily parasitised and this limits the harm they can do. Orange growers who rely on carnivorous ants maintain a better balance of species in their orchards. In the long run, ants do a lot less damage than chemicals.
