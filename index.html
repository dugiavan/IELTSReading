Learning to Walk

These days the feet of a typical city dweller rarely encounter terrain more uneven than a crack in the pavement. While that may not seem like a problem, it turns out that by flattening our urban environment we have put ourselves at risk of a surprising number of chronic illnesses and disabilities. Fortunately, the commercial market has come to the rescue with a choice of products.

Research into the idea that flat floors could be detrimental to our health was pioneered back in the late 1960s. Podiatrist Charles Brantingham and physiologist Bruce Beekman were concerned with the growing epidemic of high blood pressure and reckoned they might be linked to the uniformity of the surfaces we walk on.

The trouble, they believed, was that walking continuously on flat floors concentrates forces on just a few areas of the foot. As a result, these surfaces are far more conducive to chronic stress syndromes than natural surfaces. They understood that the anatomy of the foot parallels that of the human hand and that modern lifestyles waste all this potential flexibility.

Brantingham and Beekman became convinced that the damage could be rectified by making people wobble. They tested this by having factory workers stand on spongy mats with varying degrees of resistance. This simple intervention made a huge difference; it activated a host of muscles in their legs, which in turn helped pump blood back to the heart and stress for circulation.

Earlier this year, John Fisher and colleagues designed a mat intended to replicate the effect of walking on cobblestones. After 16 weeks, participants showed marked improvements in mobility and significant reduction in blood pressure. The mats are now available for purchase and production is being scaled up.

In China, spas and factories promote cobblestone paths as healthful amenities. People in Europe are also embracing the idea, visiting "barefoot parks" to walk on mud, logs, and moss. If none of these appeal, a new shoe called Masai Barefoot Technology offers another option. These shoes have rounded soles that cause you to rock slightly when standing still, training the small muscles around the ankles responsible for stability.

If the urban environment is detrimental to our health, a far simpler alternative is spending time in the countryside. However, for many citizens, the countryside is a dwindling resource as concrete cities grow at a terrifying rate.

Yawning

When Robert R Provine began studying yawning in the 1960s, it was difficult for him to convince research students of the merits of ‘yawning science’. Although it may appear quirky, his research was a logical extension of developmental neuroscience.

The verb ‘to yawn’ is derived from the Old English ganien, meaning to gape or open wide. Provine studied yawns by using a variation of the contagion response, asking people to 'think about yawning' to trigger the act.

Provine’s early discoveries: the yawn is highly stereotyped but not invariant in its duration. It is an excellent example of the instinctive fixed action pattern. Once started, a yawn progresses with the inevitability of a sneeze.

There are no half-yawns; this is why you cannot stifle a yawn. Yawns can come in bouts with a highly variable inter-yawn interval of about 68 seconds.

To understand its function, he tested three informative variants:
1. The closed nose yawn: Subjects could still perform normal yawns, proving the nostrils are not essential.
2. The clenched teeth yawn: By asking subjects to clench their teeth, they felt "stuck" mid-yawn. This shows that gaping of the jaws is a necessary component.
3. The nose yawn: Unlike normal breathing, yawning is impossible via nasal inhalation alone.

Yawning is a powerful movement that involves stretching facial muscles, tilting the head back, and even producing tears. It often shares properties with stretching, forming a "global motor complex".

A fascinating observation involves stroke patients paralyzed on one side. When they yawn, their paralyzed arm may rise and flex automatically—an associated response. This suggests yawning activates unconscious connections between the brain and the motor system.

Ultimately, yawning seems associated with a change of behavioral state, switching from one activity to another. It is a reminder of the ancient behavior that lurks beneath the veneer of culture and language.

Endangered Languages

‘Nevermind whales, save the languages’, says Peter Monaghan. Worried about the loss of rain forests and the ozone layer? Well, neither of those is doing any worse than the 6,000 to 7,000 languages that remain in use on Earth. One half of the survivors will almost certainly be gone by 2050, while 40% more will probably be well on their way out. In their place, almost all humans will speak one of a handful of megalanguages – Mandarin, English, Spanish.

Linguists know what causes languages to disappear, but less often remarked is what happens on the way to disappearance: languages’ vocabularies, grammars and expressive potential all diminish. When a community goes over from a traditional language to a creole, they leave behind a very fine vocabulary for the landscape. As speakers become less able to express the wealth of knowledge that has filled ancestors’ lives, communities tend to become demoralised.

If the losses are so huge, why are relatively few linguists combating the situation? Australian linguists have achieved a great deal in terms of preserving languages, resulting in good documentation. In England, Peter Austin has directed efforts to limit language loss. However, there is growing evidence that not all approaches will be particularly helpful. Some linguists boast of more and more sophisticated means of capturing languages: digital recording and storage.

But these encourage the "quick dash" style of recording trip. That’s not quite what some specialists have been seeking for 30 years. Michael Krauss complains that linguists are playing with non-essentials while most of their raw data is disappearing. Who is to blame? Many blame those obsessed with Noam Chomsky’s approaches. Theoretical linguists have had so much influence that the profession has largely ignored the continuing disappearance of languages.

Chomsky’s landmark work of 1957 argues that all languages exhibit certain grammatical features, encoded in the human mind. Creating dictionaries and grammars requires prolonged and dedicated work. Documentary linguists must observe structural subtleties, but also social and political factors. Such work calls for persistent funding for field scientists who may venture into harsh and even hazardous places.

Once there, they may face difficulties such as community suspicion. Parents may give up using the language with their children, believing they will benefit from speaking a more widely understood one. The highest barrier to language preservation is the profession's emphasis on theory, which wears down the enthusiasm of linguists. While Chomsky has recently spoken in support of preservation, his argument remains unsentimental: the loss of a language is a tragedy because it means the permanent loss of the most relevant data. However, Nick Evans objects, arguing that the process of immersion in a language is the most demanding intellectual task.

The Ant and the Mandarin

In 1476, the farmers of Berne in Switzerland decided there was only one way to rid their fields of the cutworms attacking their crops. They took the pests to court. The worms were tried, found guilty and excommunicated by the arch­bishop. In China, farmers had a more practical approach to pest control. Rather than relying on divine intervention, they put their faith in frogs, ducks and ants.

Frogs and ducks were encouraged to snap up the pests in the paddies and the occasional plague of locusts. But the notion of biological control began with an ant. More specifically, it started with the predatory yellow citrus ant, which has been polishing off pests in the orange groves of southern China for ít nhất 1,700 years. The yellow citrus ant is a type of weaver ant, which binds leaves and twigs with silk to form a neat, tent-like nest. In the beginning, farmers made do with the odd ants’ nests here and there. But it wasn’t long before growing demand led to the development of a thriving trade in nests and a new type of agriculture – ant farming.

For an insect that bites, the yellow citrus ant is remarkably popular. Even by ant standards, it is a fearsome predator. It’s big, runs fast and has a powerful nip – painful to humans but lethal to many of the insects that plague the orange groves. For ít nhất 17 centuries, Chinese orange growers have harnessed những six-legged killing machines to keep their fruit groves healthy and productive.

Citrus fruits evolved in the Far East and the Chinese discovered the delights of their flesh early on. As the ancestral home of oranges, lemons and pomelos, China also has the greatest diversity of citrus pests. And the trees that produce the sweetest fruits, the mandarins – or kan – attract a host of plant-eating insects, from sap-sucking mealy bugs to leaf-devouring caterpillars. Với so many enemies, fruit growers clearly had to have some way of protecting their orchards.

The West did not discover the Chinese orange growers’ secret weapon cho đến the early 20th century. At the time, Florida was suffering an epidemic of citrus canker. Walter Swingle, a plant physiologist working for the US Department of Agriculture, was sent to China in search of varieties of orange that were resistant to the disease. Swingle spent some time studying the citrus orchards around Guangzhou, and there he came across the story of the cultivated ant. These ants, he was told, were “grown” by the people of a small village nearby who sold them to the orange growers by the nestful.

The earliest report of citrus ants at work among the orange trees appeared in a book on botany written by Hsi Han in AD 304. “The people of Chiao-Chih sell in their markets ants in bags of rush matting. In the south, if the kan trees do not have this kind of ant, the fruits will all be damaged by many harmful insects, and not a single fruit will be perfect.” Initially, farmers relied on nests collected from the wild or bought in the market where trade in nests was brisk.

The business quickly became more sophisticated. From the 10th century, country people began to trap ants in artificial nests baited with fat. “Fruit-growing families buy these ants from vendors who make a business of collecting and selling such creatures,” wrote Chuang Chi-Yu in 1130. They wait until the ants have migrated into the bladders and take them away. By the 17th century, growers were building bamboo walkways between their trees to speed the colonisation of their orchards.

The orange growers clearly thought it worked. One authority stressed how essential it was to keep the fruit trees free of insect pests. “But hand labour is not nearly as efficient as ant power…” Swingle was just as impressed. Yet despite his reports, many Western biologists were sceptical. In the West, the idea of using one insect to destroy another was new and highly controversial. The first breakthrough had come in 1888, when the infant orange industry in California had been saved từ extinction by the Australian vedalia beetle.

The long tradition of ants in Chinese orchards only began to waver in the 1950s and 1960s with the introduction of powerful organic insecticides. Although most fruit growers switched to chemicals, those who abandoned ants in favour of chemicals quickly became disillusioned. As costs soared and pests began to develop resistance to the chemicals, growers began to revive the old ant patrols. They had good reason to have faith in their insect workforce.

Research showed that as long as there were enough ants, they did an excellent job of dispatching some pests. Trees with yellow ants produced almost 20 per cent more healthy leaves. More recent trials have shown that những trees yield just as big a crop as những protected by expensive chemical sprays.

One apparent drawback was that citrus ants protect mealy bugs in exchange for the sweet honey-dew they secrete. Where mealy bugs proliferate under the ants’ protection, they are usually heavily parasitised and this limits the harm they can do. Orange growers who rely on carnivorous ants maintain a better balance of species in their orchards. In the long run, ants do a lot less damage than chemicals.

The Alaskan permafrost is thawing

The Alaskan permafrost is thawing fast, releasing three trillion gallons of water per year than nó đã từng làm just four decades ago. A Wisconsin-sized region of frozen soil is disappearing, raising concerns about unleashing new pathogens on humanity.

Thawing permafrost is among climate science’s worst “positive feedback loops”: As the world warms, permafrost—essentially frozen soil—thaws, releasing fresh water and carbon into the environment. That release further fuels climate change, driving more warming.

In a new study, researchers tracked how thawing permafrost in the North Slope region has added fresh water và dissolved organic carbon vào các estuaries off the Alaskan coast. In recent years, the region released nearly 12 cubic kilometers mỗi năm, enough to fill more than 4.5 million Olympic swimming pools.

Fresh water là một problem for the ocean—it disrupts sea ice formation, alters the salinity của các coastal lagoons và vịnh, và có thể threaten global ocean circulation. Từ đầu những năm 1980 đến 2023, the amount of carbon mà các dòng sông ở phía bắc Alaska thải ra đã risen từ khoảng 120 gigagrams lên tới khoảng 170 gigagrams.

“Một phần lượng carbon đó đã been locked up trong tens of thousands of years. Giờ đây nó đã tan ra, mobilized trong các dòng sông, và đi ra đại dương,” Michael Rawlins, lead author of the study, cho biết. “Outgassing”—sự giải phóng methane hoặc carbon dioxide—có thể happen from rivers directly trước khi nó reaches the ocean.

Nghiên cứu có thể giúp các nhà khoa học better understand permafrost-fueled carbon emissions trên khắp the Arctic, một khu vực đang warming about three times faster than phần còn lại của hành tinh. “Khi chúng ta cố gắng hiểu rõ hơn về lượng carbon trong đại dương, chúng ta cần good estimates về lượng carbon coming out of these rivers,” Rawlins nói thêm.

Các findings, được published in the journal Global Biogeochemical Cycles, highlight cái critical moment đối với khoa học. Chưa bao giờ có một thời điểm quan trọng hơn để stand up và cho thấy why science matters.

What psychedelics actually do to the brain

Psychedelics can make people experience themselves and the world in striking ways—forms morph and sounds distort. Some even experience “ego death,” a fleeting condition where they lose their sense of self. But what happens inside the brain to cause such sensations isn’t fully understood.

A new comprehensive analysis reveals that psychedelics generate a high degree of chatter between regions of the brain involved in sensory perception and those for thinking. “Usually, the brain’s perception of the external world is very distinct from our memory and abstract thinking,” says neuroscientist Manesh Girn.

Understanding psychedelics has become increasingly important as interest in their ability to treat mental health conditions like anxiety and depression explodes. Researchers hope to optimize their healing potential. Since initial research began more than fifty years ago, before the advent of effective brain scanning technology like fMRI, scientists are working to catch up now.

Prior imaging studies have been small, leading to inconsistent findings. For example, some highly emphasized disruptions to the brain’s default mode network—the area responsible for defining our identity. This new research applied a standardized framework to analyze data from more than 500 scans, making it a tour de force in the field.

The analysis confirmed that these compounds don’t impact a single part of the brain but fundamentally change how the brain is processing information. This expanded integration may break down rigid cognitive patterns and increase flexibility of thought, which could help patients with addictive disorders.

Importantly, unusual activity cũng xuất hiện ở các vùng não involved in movement. This may explain why một số physical symptoms of Parkinson’s disease improved in clinical settings. Tuy nhiên, scientists say much more research is needed to characterize the full range of experiences, from visual experiences to emotional processing.

While this offers strong evidence, we are still in the early stages. As Girn says, “We have barely even scratched the surface.”

April Showers to Storms

After months of cold, April brings brightness to the Northern Hemisphere. Rain helps seeds germinate, brings flower blooms, and refills drinking water supplies. But too much can hinder spring planting, lead to major flooding, and damage infrastructure.

Researchers say April showers are transforming from sprinkles into heavier storms as Earth’s temperature rises. Since the 1950s, heavier rain events have become more frequent and intense across most of the United States. As a result, communities are dealing with unprecedented heavy rain events. Last April, for example, flash flooding and tornadoes pounded several states, dropping a record three months’ worth of precipitation in days. The historic round of severe weather claimed 24 lives; 15 were linked to flooding.

The link between warmer temperatures and rainfall is relatively straightforward, as a warmer atmosphere can “hold” more water. For every degree Fahrenheit that air temperature rises, the atmosphere can hold 4% more water. While no weather is caused by climate change, warmer conditions are creating a more water-loaded environment for storms to thrive. Climate change is also affecting the flow of energy. As water evaporates, it pulls heat from the surface. When the vapor condenses into clouds, it releases that energy.

“You can think of it as a bigger bucket,” says Jonathan Winter, a climate scientist. When an intense thunderstorm comes through to squeeze out the atmosphere, you have that bigger bucket to empty. Warmer ocean temperatures also increase evaporation, transferring nhiều moisture to the air. In some parts of the U.S., the Northeast has experienced about 60% more rain than six decades ago. This collision of warm, moist air and cool air causes extreme atmospheric instability.

It is possible to anticipate the areas that will be more vulnerable to flooding. The most susceptible places are those that are low-lying or close to a floodplain. To adapt to những thay đổi này, town planners are resizing culverts that channel water and building more resilient bridges. They are even preemptively moving people away từ các khu vực flood-prone.

The Shifting Tornado Alley

Tornadoes are some of the most destructive natural disasters seen in the U.S. each year, and twisters are 10 times more common in Tornado Alley. But for tornado expert Stephen Strader, the term may be a bit outdated. “We've had devastating tornadoes outside of what people consider Tornado Alley,” he explains. And the disastrous phenomenon is only expected to get worse.

Drier conditions in the Great Plains, coupled with increased moisture in the Southeast, is shifting tornado activity away from states like Texas and Oklahoma. Scientists attribute this shift to climate change. “It’s a very subtle shift,” Strader says. Still, the shift is “notable,” says Jana Houser. “We are seeing places that are not expecting tornadoes become victims to tornadoes.” In 2024, six different tornado outbreaks caused billions of dollars in damage each.

How do tornadoes form?

Strader compares it to baking a cake. You need specific ingredients: heat, moisture, drier air, and wind shear (changes in wind speed and direction that create rotation). The dry air from the Rocky Mountains forms what’s called a “cap” that slows the development of a tornado. As climate change increases, that cap is getting stronger, meaning storms that used to occur in Oklahoma may now start in Central Arkansas. “It basically means you're gonna have to bake your cake longer.”

What states are in Tornado Alley?

Tornado Alley spreads across the Great Plains and Midwest. In 1948, two Air Force officers correctly predicted a tornado would hit an Air Force Base, marking the start of modern tornado warnings. Four years later, they first used the term “Tornado Alley.” Even though its boundaries are shifting, experts say the traditional area isn't going away because the Rocky Mountains and the Gulf of Mexico are critical factors.

The rising risk in the Southeast

This eastern shift poses distinct risks. The Southeast has a larger population, meaning tornadoes hit more developed areas, increasing fatalities. The region also has more trees that reduce visibility. Night tornadoes are more frequent here and are twice as deadly because people may miss critical alerts while asleep. The region also has many mobile homes, which aren’t designed to withstand tornadoes.

Societal elements and Climate Change

Climate change isn't the only factor; population growth and urbanization play a huge role. As cities expand, more infrastructure is placed in harm's way. “The tornado that was in the middle of a field 50 years ago is now going through a brand-new subdivision,” Strader explains. This creates a multiplicative effect, making impacts grow exponentially.

Prediction and Safety

Researchers are using AI and computer modeling to improve prediction. To stay safe at home, find an interior room on the lowest level or a basement. If driving, look for a ditch or low-lying area and avoid parking under bridges. Everyone should have a NOAA Weather Radio, which alerts you even during sleep. Tornado sirens, originally for Cold War bomb raids, were never meant to be heard indoors, so you can't rely on them.

Ahead of Its Time

A chance discovery in New Zealand has challenged the country’s recorded history.

One October afternoon, a young New Zealander, Sam Tobin, called his dogs and went for a walk down to the nearby Ruamahanga River. Having been very high for days, the river had at last fallen, and Tobin was eager to see what changes the floods had brought. The family farm borders the river and a four-metre-high flood bank testifies to its natural tendency to flood.

Tobin stepped out onto a broad shoulder of river sand, where he noticed what he initially took to be a whitish rock, lit by the sun. Then, getting closer, he realised it was a bone. Such a thing was not uncommon in những vùng này - he had often come across bone fragments, or even whole skeletons, of cows and sheep. But as he scraped aside the stones he realised it was a human bone, something quite new in his experience. As he picked it up, he saw it was a skull, discoloured with age.

Tobin replaced the skull and hurried home to tell his mother what the river had delivered to their doorstep. It would prove to be a spectacular find, setting in motion an investigation by some of the country’s most respected specialists, and ultimately challenging our most firmly held assertions about the human settlement of New Zealand.

The police were immediately called, but despite a thorough search could find nothing that might shed light on the identity of the Ruamahanga skull, or the circumstances of its sudden appearance. The skull was then taken north to be examined by forensic pathologist Dr Ferris, at Auckland Hospital. Despite being hampered by its damaged condition - the jawbone and lower left portion of the cranium were missing - Dr Ferris determined that the skull was that of a female. He then consulted with a colleague, Dr Koelmeyer, who believed that the deterioration of the bone placed the time of death before living memory and, most significantly as it would turn out, the skull appeared to be European in origin.

Wellington-based forensic anthropologist Dr Watt also examined the skull, and suggested it belonged to a 40-45 year-old. He believed that it could be the remains of an old farm burial, but was not certain, and proposed the use of radiocarbon dating to make sure it wasn’t a recent death. As a result, the Institute of Geological and Nuclear Sciences (GNS) was contacted, and provided with a sample of bone. In a little over three weeks the seemingly astonishing results came back. Cutting through the bewildering complexity of the scientific analysis was a single line reading: conventional radiocarbon age approximately 296 years. This was staggering, for the skull was about 200 years older than Dr Koelmeyer had believed.

Of course, a skull of this age wasn’t particularly unusual in New Zealand. The Maori people have been living in the country for at least 800 years and scientists frequently come across human remains of considerable age. The fascinating question, however, was how a skull of this race, let alone this gender, had reached những hòn đảo xa xôi này long before the arrival of the explorer Captain Cook in 1769, and perhaps even before the very first European landfall – the fleeting visit of the Dutch explorer Tasman in 1642 – neither of whom had women among their crews.

The first known European women in the Pacific came with a doomed colonising venture which sailed from Peru in 1595 under the command of Spanish captain Mendana. However, it is unlikely the Ruamahanga skull originated from this expedition because no evidence of Mendana’s ships has ever been found in New Zealand, while a team of archaeologists working in the Solomon Islands in 1970 did discover the remains of European vessels dating from the 16th century.

Two centuries were to pass before the first recorded European females arrived in New Zealand, both having escaped from prison in Australia. How do we account for the Ruamahanga skull, which appears to be about 100 years older than that? It is impossible to say with certainty, but the most likely explanation is that a Spanish or Portuguese trading-ship was washed onto these wild shores as a result of a shipwreck and a woman got ashore. Implausible, perhaps, but the Ruamahanga skull could be the kind of concrete evidence that demands such a re-evaluation of history.

Mind Music

A. Ever had a song stuck in your head, playing on an endless loop? Scientists call them ‘involuntary musical images’, or ‘earworms’, and a wave of new research is shining light on why they occur and what can be learned from them. Some neuroscientists and cognitive psychologists are studying earworms to explore the mysteries of memory and the part of the brain that is beyond our conscious control. ‘The ý tưởng rằng chúng ta có toàn quyền kiểm soát quá trình tư duy là an illusion,’ says psychologist Lauren Stewart. Researchers haven’t been able to watch what happens in the brain because they happen unpredictably. Much of what is known comes from surveys, questionnaires, diaries and lab experiments.

B. A study showed that the singing we hear in our heads tends to be true to actual recordings. Researchers had 17 volunteers tap to the beat of any earworm. The tapping tempos were within 10% of the tempos of the original recordings. Another study found that people who find them most intrusive, have slightly different brain structures, with more gray matter in areas associated with processing emotions.

C. Studies also show that the music in our heads often starts playing during times of ‘low cognitive load’, such as while showering or doing chores. Dr Stewart likens earworms to ‘sonic screen savers’ that keep the mind entertained while it is otherwise unoccupied. She observed that the more challenging the activity, the less likely the volunteers were to hear the music. Songs tend to match people’s moods and therefore they are not random. If you are energized and upbeat, an earworm is likely to be uptempo too.

D. Songs the brain fixates on are thường là những bài nó been exposed to recently, which is why tunes getting heavy radio play frequently top the earworm charts. Even tunes you didn’t pay attention to can worm their way into your subconscious.

E. Some earworms are just fragments of a song that repeat like a broken record. So, when the mind hits a part of a song it can’t remember, it loops back rather than moving on. That could make an earworm even more entrenched. According to a theory known as the Zeigarnik effect, unfinished thoughts and activities weigh on the mind more heavily than those that are completed.

F. Researchers say they can’t pinpoint a spot in the brain where earworms live. Imaging studies have shown that deliberately imagining music and actually listening to music activate many of the same neurological networks. Subjects listening to familiar music activated the supplementary motor area, which is typically involved in remembering sequences.

G. One factor that makes some songs stick might be repetition. ‘Repetition leads to familiarity which leads to anticipation,’ says John Seabrook. Producers pump pop songs full of aural hooks, the punchy melodic phrases designed to target the brain. Songs với tiềm năng thành earworm thường chia sẻ các đặc điểm: a repeating pattern of ups and downs in pitch, and an 塑造 irregular musical interval.

H. The researchers plan next to test their results in reverse. They are looking at whether people at different stages of life experience earworms differently. ‘Older people might get them more often because they know more songs,’ Dr Halpern says. ‘But the few responses we have so far indicate that they have earworms less often.’

READING PASSAGE 11
Information Theory

Information theory lies at the heart of everything – from DVD players and the genetic code of DNA to the physics of the universe at its most fundamental. It has been central to the development of the science of communication, which enables data to be sent electronically and has therefore had a major impact on our lives.

A. In April 2002 an event took place which demonstrated one of the many applications of information theory. The space probe, Voyager I, launched in 1977, had sent back spectacular images of Jupiter and Saturn and then soared out of the Solar System on a one-way mission to the stars. After 25 years of exposure to the freezing temperatures of deep space, the probe was beginning to show its age. Sensors and circuits were on the brink of failing and NASA experts realised that they had to do something or lose contact with their probe forever. The solution was to instruct it to use spares to change the failing parts. With the probe 12 billion kilometres from Earth, this was not an easy task. By means of a radio dish, the message was sent out into the depths of space. Yet, incredibly, the little probe managed to hear the faint call from its home planet, and successfully made the switchover.

B. It was the longest-distance repair job in history, and a triumph for the NASA engineers. But it also highlighted the astonishing power of the techniques developed by Claude Shannon. Shannon showed an early talent for maths and for building gadgets, and made breakthroughs in the foundations of computer technology. While at Bell Laboratories, Shannon developed information theory, but shunned the resulting acclaim. He single-handedly created an entire science of communication which has since inveigled its way into a host of applications – any area, in short, where data has to be conveyed rapidly yet accurately.

C. This all dường như light years away from the down-to-earth uses Shannon originally had for his work. He set out with an apparently simple aim: to pin down the precise meaning of the concept of ‘information’. The most basic form of information, Shannon argued, can be captured in the binary unit, or ‘bit’, of the form 1 or 0. Having identified this fundamental unit, Shannon set about defining otherwise vague ideas. In the process he discovered something surprising: it is always possible to guarantee information will get through random interference – ‘noise’ – intact.

D. ‘Noise’ usually means unwanted sounds which interfere with genuine information. Information theory generalises this idea via theorems that capture the effects of noise with mathematical precision. In particular, Shannon showed that noise sets a limit on the rate at which information can pass along communication channels while remaining error-free. This rate depends on the relative strengths of the signal and noise, and on its capacity (its ‘bandwidth’). The trick is to find ways of packaging up – ‘coding’ – information to cope with the ravages of noise.

E. Over the years scientists have devised many such methods, and they have proved crucial in many technological feats. The Voyager spacecraft transmitted data using codes which added one extra bit for every single bit of information; the result was an error rate of just one bit in 10,000 – and stunningly clear pictures. Other codes have become part of everyday life – such as the bar code, which uses a simple error-detecting system that ensures lasers can read the price even on a crumpled bag of crisps. Engineers made a major breakthrough by discovering so-called turbo codes – which play a key role in the mobile videophone revolution.

F. Shannon also laid the foundations of more efficient ways of storing information, by stripping out superfluous (‘redundant’) bits from data which contributed little real information. As text messages show, it is often possible to leave out a lot of data without losing much meaning. However, there’s a limit beyond which messages become too ambiguous. Shannon showed how to calculate this limit, opening the way to the thiết kế của các compression methods that cram maximum information into the minimum space.

The History of Pencil

The beginning of the story of pencils started with a lightning. Graphite, the main material for producing pencil, was discovered in 1564 when a lightning struck a local tree. Local people found out that the black substance spotted at the root of the unlucky tree was different from burning ash of wood. It was soft, thus left marks everywhere. Chemistry was barely out of its infancy at the time, so people mistook it for lead. It was soon put to use by locals in marking their sheep for ownership and calculation.

Britain turns out to be major country where mines of graphite can be detected and developed. Even so, the first pencil was invented elsewhere. As graphite is soft, it requires some form of encasement. In Italy, graphite sticks were initially wrapped in string or sheepskin for stability. Then around 1560, an Italian couple made what are likely the first blueprints for the modern, wood-encased carpentry pencil. Their concept involved the hollowing out of a stick of juniper wood. Shortly thereafter, a superior technique was discovered: two wooden halves were carved, a graphite stick inserted, and the halves then glued together.

Although graphite core in pencils is still referred to as lead, modern pencils do not contain lead. The “lead” is actually a mix of finely ground graphite and clay powders. This mixture is important because the amount of clay added depends on the intended hardness. The more clay you put in, the higher hardness the core has. Many pencils are graded on the European system. This system used B for black and H for hard; a pencil’s grade was described by a sequence of Hs or Bs. The standard writing pencil is graded HB.

In England, pencils continue to be made from whole sawn graphite. But with the mass production of pencils, they are getting drastically more popular. As demands rise, appetite for graphite soars.

According to the USGS, world production of natural graphite in 2012 was 1,100,000 tonnes. However, much in contrast with its intellectual application, graphite was also widely used in the military. It was used as a refractory material to line moulds for cannonballs, resulting in rounder, smoother balls that could be fired farther. This particular deposit of graphite was extremely pure and soft. Because of its military importance, this unique mine was strictly controlled by the Crown.

That the US did not use pencils in outer space is in fact a fiction. It is widely known that Russian astronauts used grease pencils, which don’t have breakage problem. Their counterparts in the US preferred mechanical pencils, which produced fine line, much clearer than the smudgy lines. But the lead tips broke often. A bit of graphite floating around the space capsule could find its way into machinery, causing an electrical short.

Against the backcloth of a digitalized world, the prospect of pencils dường như bleak. In reality, it does not. The application of pencils has by now become so widespread. A spectrum of users are likely to continue to use it: artists to draw on sketch pads, and architects to produce blue prints. The possibilities seem limitless.

Nanotechnology: Its Development and Uses

A. Nanotechnology has been hailed by many as being a miracle of science. Essentially, nanotechnology is engineering at the atomic level. Scientists work with nanoparticles which may measure no more than a billionth of a metre. Whilst some substances derived from carbon compounds are manufactured, others are naturally-occurring or arise as a by-product. What makes these substances of such interest is that their minute size facilitates processes that would otherwise be impossible.

B. It may be something of a revelation to learn that nanotechnology is far from cutting-edge science. In fact, the idea was first referred to in an influential lecture by Richard Feynman in 1959. Feynman outlined the basic concept. He envisaged a process involving the building of a set of precise tools to build another proportionally smaller set. The building of increasingly minute tools would in turn produce ultra-microscopic materials.

C. Strangely, what should have sparked a revolution was then virtually forgotten for 15 years. In 1974, Norio Taniguchi reintroduced Feynman’s theory. However, it wasn’t until the 1980s that the way was paved for nanotechnology to leave the realm of theoretical science and become reality. The invention of the Scanning Tunnelling Microscope, combined with the discovery of fullerenes, were to prove a turning point.

D. Fullerenes possess chemical and physical properties of huge interest. They are used as antimicrobial agents and for several biomedical applications, from X-ray imaging to treating cancer by targeting cells. They are also used in sunscreen and cosmetics. Nanoparticles are also effective in environmental clean-ups of oil slicks. Their remarkable properties are down to: their greater surface-to-weight ratio and their ability to penetrate cell membranes.

E. Despite these properties, nanotechnology has yet to win universal acceptance. The very properties that make them valuable also make them potentially toxic. Such properties are potentially lethal if toxic substances attach themselves to nanoparticles, delivering a fatal toxin into the cells. Their size permits them to enter the bloodstream. The body has no natural immune system to deal with these particles. Sceptics are not yet convinced that the potential side effects are compensated for by the advantages. However, some believe that regulations can minimise such risks, and that risks are vastly outweighed by potential benefits.

Analyzing air pollution health, economic risks from AI data centers

By Karen Feldscher | April 8, 2026

Rapid expansion of the artificial intelligence (AI) technology industry has driven a massive increase in electricity demand to power AI data centers. Many of these centers are building or planning to build on-site fossil fuel-driven power plants to alleviate strain on the local energy grid. Michael Cork—who earned a PhD in biostatistics at Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health in December 2025 and is now a postdoctoral researcher—has conducted analyses showing that air pollution from these new power sources could have serious health and economic impacts.

How did you get involved with looking at pollution from AI data centers?

I was doing a PhD in biostatistics under my adviser, Francesca Dominici. In my dissertation work I specialized in tying air pollution to health impacts using modern causal inference frameworks and large datasets such as the Medicare database, including developing new ways to estimate the causal relationship between air pollution and health impacts in the presence of complex confounding.

A couple of years ago, with so much investment happening in AI, I began to focus on how emissions from AI data centers could impact community health. It became increasingly clear to me that powering data centers was going to be a problem—we know we have to reduce emissions to avoid climate catastrophe and protect public health, but these data centers require substantial amounts of energy to run. The two things are at odds.

A lot of the conversation has centered on energy demand and the country’s grid capacity. What I think is getting less attention right now is that this is also a public health issue because of the dangers of fine particulate matter. Fine particulate matter is estimated to drive nearly 90% of the health impacts from air pollution and is often not captured in traditional energy or climate assessments.

Can you describe your research?

My research focuses on developing methods to understand how air pollution from energy infrastructure impacts population health. I estimate the real-world health costs of fine particulate matter exposure by quantifying outcomes such as hospital admissions, respiratory illness, and premature mortality.

Together with Dr. Dominici, I co-founded EmPower Analytics Group, a consulting company that provides independent, science-based health and economic analyses of AI infrastructure. These reports enable community members, regulators, and industry players to better understand the health effects.

What’s involved with figuring out the health and economic costs?

There are a few challenges. One is to try to understand how much pollution leaves a plant, because often we don’t have comprehensive data on how often these plants are running. The next challenge is understanding where the pollution might travel. Air pollution doesn’t respect any borders, so we need to think about the appropriate dispersion model.

Translating an increase in air pollution to an increase in health risks is also challenging. We also need to consider high-risk groups, such as children, the elderly, and people with pre-existing conditions. Social determinants of health all shape how vulnerable you are to additional pollution exposure.

What are some of the analyses you’ve conducted?

Together with the Piedmont Environmental Council, we analyzed the Vantage Data Center in Loudoun County, Virginia. We estimated between $53 million and $99 million in annual health damages—among the largest health-damage estimates produced for a single facility to date. The damages are driven primarily by an estimated 3.4 to 6.5 additional premature deaths per year, along with lost productivity.

In another case, we looked at the proposed xAI Gas Plant in Mississippi. We projected an increase in regional air pollution in an area that already carries substantial air pollution burden. We estimated that the most impacted people would be those with high social vulnerability.

Do you see ways to reconcile these massive needs with health costs?

The first thing I’d say is that we can’t solve a problem we haven’t measured. Right now, permitting decisions are being made without any clear accounting of their public health costs. On the technical side, some companies are exploring ways to shift computational load across data center networks—routing work to facilities powered by renewables like solar or wind. This kind of demand-side flexibility could meaningfully reduce emissions.

None of these solutions are simple, but the first step is illuminating these impacts so that health and environmental costs are treated as real costs, not externalities to be ignored. Once those costs are visible, it becomes much easier to align on better solutions.

Malaria-transmitting mosquitoes in South America evolving to evade insecticides

By Maya Brownstein | March 26, 2026

Boston, MA—Anopheles darlingi mosquitoes—a major vector of malaria in South America—are evolving in response to insecticides, which may make them harder to kill and malaria more difficult to control, according to a new study led by Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health.

The study, published March 26, 2026, in Science, is the first to sequence a large number (>1000) of complete genomes of Anopheles mosquitoes in the Americas, where there are more than 600,000 cases of malaria annually, mostly in Brazil, Colombia, and Venezuela.

“Malaria remains stubbornly persistent in South America, and there is a risk that dangerous drug-resistant strains of the malaria parasite could evolve in the Americas and then spread elsewhere,” said corresponding author Jacob Tennessen. “Our study plays a major role in revealing the evolutionary dynamics of a primary malaria vector, providing new insights into Anopheles darlingi biology that could help improve methods for blocking disease transmission.”

Research Methodology

Prior studies on Anopheles darlingi population genetics have used sets of genetic markers but not the whole genome. For this study, the researchers generated whole genome sequences for 1,094 adult female mosquitoes from 16 locations—including forests, wetlands, grasslands, farming and mining areas, and cities—across six South American countries: French Guiana, Brazil, Guyana, Peru, Venezuela, and Colombia.

Key Findings: Evading Insecticides

The study found that Anopheles darlingi are evolving to evade insecticides—a novel result. “Insecticide resistance has only been sporadically documented in Anopheles darlingi,” Tennessen said. “We were not expecting to see resistance-related genes evolving as much as we did. Resistance may be driven by agricultural insecticides rather than those used for vector control specifically.”

The researchers also found extensive genetic divergence among Anopheles darlingi mosquitoes across the continent and observed that the species is well poised to adapt to changes in its environment.

A Milestone for Vector Biology

According to the researchers, the study is a milestone for vector biology in the Americas and provides a template for future studies of other Anopheles species in the region. While the study findings contribute to a knowledge base that can inform malaria control efforts, senior author Daniel Neafsey noted, “this was basic research rather than an applied study. Additional research is required before any policy changes are implemented.”

Environmental Impacts of Air Pollution

TL;DR: Air pollutants—ranging from greenhouse gases and ozone to aerosols like black carbon—affect not only human health but also ecosystems, the water cycle, and the global climate. They contribute to acid rain, ocean acidification, ozone depletion, and shifts in Earth's albedo, affecting climate change and disrupting biodiversity. While some pollutants temporarily mask global warming, lasting solutions require comprehensive emission reductions.

Air pollution is both widespread and varied. While many air pollution emissions—mostly man-made—harm human health, air pollution’s effects on the environment and climate are equally concerning.

Wildlife faces many of the same challenges that air pollution poses for humans. Animals often suffer from air pollution’s adverse health effects and national parks can experience the same visibility issues that cities face from smog and particulate matter. However, air pollution’s effects on the environment extend beyond these examples, affecting ecosystems in multiple ways.

How air pollution affects the hydrologic (water) cycle

The hydrologic cycle refers to the continuous circulation of water through the atmosphere and along the ground. Air pollution affects the hydrologic cycle in the form of acid rain and ocean acidification.

Acid rain

Acid rain is caused by two air pollutants: sulfur dioxide (SO2) and nitrogen oxides (NOx), both of which are usually emitted by man-made sources. NOx, in particular, is commonly emitted by cars and contributes to photochemical smog, while SO2 emissions often come from electric power generators. When SO2 and NOx react with the water in the atmosphere, they form sulfuric and nitric acids, respectively. These acids then fall to the ground in the form of rain, or other acid deposition, such as snow, hail, fog, or even sometimes as dry deposition, such as dust.

Acid deposition has negative impacts on various ecosystems. When acid rain flows through soil, it causes aluminum to leach out from the dirt and into water runoff. Then, when the acid rain flows into bodies of water, it increases the water’s acidity and aluminum levels, harming many of the fish and wildlife that live there and reducing biodiversity.

Acid rain can leach away certain nutrients from the soil, damaging plant health. However, nitrogen oxides and ammonia can also cause nitrogen deposition, leading to the over-enrichment of soil and the eutrophication of bodies of water. The overabundance of nitrogen harms certain plants and promotes algae growth, reducing biodiversity and disrupting ecosystems. Sulfur dioxide also has a harmful effect on vegetation, and acid fog can strip nutrients from plants such as evergreen trees directly without entering the soil.

Ocean acidification

Ocean acidification refers to a gradual reduction in the pH of the ocean. It is sometimes called “climate change’s equally evil twin.” This phenomenon mainly occurs because of the increasing levels of carbon dioxide (CO2) in the atmosphere from anthropogenic activities such as the burning of fossil fuels and land use changes like deforestation.

Roughly 30% of the carbon dioxide released into the atmosphere is absorbed by the ocean. Since the industrial era, the ocean has absorbed about 525 billion tons of CO2 and currently absorbs about 22 million tons per day. Carbon dioxide causes a series of chemical reactions that make the ocean’s water more acidic, harming many forms of aquatic life. In the last 200 years, the ocean has already grown acidic faster than any other known change in ocean chemistry over the past 50 million years.

Although the effects of ocean acidification on aquatic life are largely unknown, it may cause many species to go extinct and greatly disrupt the delicate ecosystems and biodiversity of the ocean as we know it. Acidic waters make it difficult for certain species, such as clownfish, to detect predators. CO2 increases the amount of hydrogen ions and decreases the amount of carbonate ions in the water. Carbonate ions are vital components in seashells and coral skeletons. Their decrease directly harms shellfish, corals, and other species that rely on them.

How ozone affects the environment

Ozone (O3) can be either helpful or harmful to the environment depending on where it is located. Ozone in the upper atmosphere (stratospheric ozone) is naturally occurring and reduces the amount of damaging UV radiation that reaches the Earth’s surface. Ozone at ground level (tropospheric ozone) is more harmful. It forms from the chemical reaction between volatile organic compounds and nitrogen oxides, two anthropogenic air pollutants.

Ground-level ozone and the environment

Tropospheric ozone harms the environment by reducing photosynthesis in plants. This air pollutant is absorbed through tiny pores on the bottom of leaves called stomata. Ozone can damage the stomata, or even cause them to close, making it more difficult for plants to take in the carbon dioxide they need to survive. Ozone can also damage the parts of leaf cells that make the necessary sugars.

In addition, ground-level ozone can slow plant growth, and make plants more vulnerable to disease, insect damage, and other threats. These negative effects have led to billions of dollars in crop loss. The National Center for Atmospheric Research has created “ozone gardens” in which ozone-related plant damage can be studied and viewed in action.

Stratospheric ozone and the environment

Stratospheric ozone protects Earth from UVB radiation. Unfortunately, certain air pollutants such as chlorine and bromine can deplete stratospheric ozone. Many anthropogenic emissions of ozone-depleting chemicals release these into the upper atmosphere. These emissions come from refrigeration and propellant devices and processes.

In addition to causing millions of deaths and negatively affecting human and animal health, UVB radiation at the Earth’s surface impacts various ecosystems. UVB radiation can cause crop and forest damage, affecting both the physiological and developmental processes of plants.

In marine ecosystems, UVB radiation harms phytoplankton, directly reducing their numbers. It also harms fish, crabs, amphibians, and other marine animals at early stages of development and decreases their reproductive capacity.

How air pollution impacts global warming and climate change

Climate change refers to long-term changes in Earth's average weather patterns. This term encompasses both global warming and its broader consequences.

Global warming refers specifically to the long-term heating of Earth’s surface. Since the 1950s, the current heating trend has clearly been a result of human activity.

Global warming is already causing profound effects worldwide. These impacts include extreme weather such as droughts and heavy rainfall. Flooding will become a greater concern. Both wildfires and storms will become more frequent and severe. Sea levels will rise and Arctic ice will melt. Ecosystems will be disrupted as plants and animals struggle to adapt to new climate conditions.

Greenhouse gases

Greenhouse gases trap heat in the atmosphere. They absorb energy and slow the rate at which that energy escapes into space. This insulating effect contributes to global warming. Main greenhouse gases include CO2, methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), and fluorinated gases. Each has different global warming potentials depending on their radiative efficiency and lifetimes in the atmosphere.

Human activities are largely responsible for the increase in greenhouse gases. The transportation sector, through fossil fuel combustion, is the largest source of direct greenhouse gas emissions.

The albedo effect

Certain air pollutants can have a more nuanced effect on climate change due to the albedo effect. Albedo refers to the proportion of light reflected by an object. Earth’s global albedo helps determine how much solar energy is reflected back out into space. A higher albedo means more reflectivity, and thus less energy and heat are absorbed. Lower albedo has the opposite effect.

For example, the presence of ice caps reduces warming because they increase albedo. However, their melting accelerates global warming because their absence reduces the earth’s albedo.

Aerosols refer to microscopic particles suspended in the atmosphere. They can affect the climate in different ways depending on whether they reflect light or absorb heat.

Instead of solving the problem, these particles merely mask it—leaving potential for greater fallout in the future. The solution to global warming lies in reducing CO2 emissions.

On the other hand, black carbon particles reduce albedo. Black carbon, also known as soot, is a type of particulate matter produced from fires and incomplete combustion. Not only does it prevent sunlight from reaching the ground, but its dark color and ability to absorb solar heat also allow it to warm the air.

While black carbon only has an atmospheric lifetime of a few weeks, its warming impact is 460-1,500 times stronger than CO2 per unit mass. Black carbon also plays a role in reducing the albedo of glaciers, eventually causing them to shrink.

Looking forward

Tackling air pollution protects both human health and the environment. However, the first step in reducing pollution is understanding it clearly.
